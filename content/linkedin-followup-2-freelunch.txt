Every "buffer ETF" is built on a trade-off the brochure never spells out. Once you see the option math, you can't unsee it.

These funds promise a defined outcome: a buffer (say, the first 15% of losses absorbed) and a cap (say, around 17% upside). Clean, reassuring, and increasingly popular - the category is now north of $80 billion.

Here's what's actually under the wrapper: four FLEX option contracts. The buffer is a put spread, and a put spread costs money. To pay for it, the fund sells away your upside - it writes a call. And the cap you get quoted? It is, almost literally, the strike price of that sold call.

So the cap and the buffer are not two separate features. They are two sides of one transaction. The protection exists because the upside was sold to fund it. There is no version of this where you get the cushion for free.

That is why any analysis - human or AI - that describes the downside protection without naming its cost (the capped upside, the forgone dividends, the fee) is selling a free lunch that does not exist. The cost isn't a footnote. It's the mechanism.

(And those stated terms? They only hold if you bought on day one of the outcome period. What a mid-period buyer actually gets is a different number - but that's a thread for another post.)
