I keep circling the same question here - whether AI can really replace the analyst, or only sound like it. The honest test, I've found, is quieter than the headline.

It isn't whether a model can summarize a fund. It's whether it can notice what the fund doesn't say.

Take a buffer ETF. The brochure makes a gentle promise: a cushion against the first stretch of losses, and a ceiling on the gains. It reads like a gift. But the two halves are the same breath. Under the wrapper sit four option contracts, and the cushion - a put spread - has to be paid for. So the fund quietly sells your upside to cover it. That ceiling you were quoted is, almost to the dollar, the price at which your gains were sold away.

Nothing was given. The protection exists because the view was sold to buy it. There is no version of this where the cushion is free.

And that, to me, is the whole line between an analyst and a brochure. The brochure says "downside protected" and stops. The analyst names the cost - the capped upside, the dividends left on the table, the fee - because the cost is the truth of the thing. An AI you'd actually trust with this work has to stand on the analyst's side of that line.

So I made it the auto-fail at the heart of my eval: any memo that claims the protection without pricing what was given up to get it fails, automatically. I call it the free-lunch gate - because a protection with no named cost is a free lunch, and a free lunch is the one thing markets never serve.

That's the project, really: turning "can you trust an AI to do this?" into a question with a real answer. Runnable, open-source, every number traced back to a real SEC filing. Repo in the comments.
