Most people picture an ETF as a basket of stocks. A lot of them are quietly full of something else: swaps.

Leveraged and inverse ETFs are built almost entirely on them. Synthetic funds replicate an index through one. Currency-hedged funds use them. Bond funds hold them to manage risk. And every one of those swaps is a private deal between the fund and a bank - agreed bilaterally, booked separately on each side.

That creates a quiet risk a stock trade doesn't have: the two sides can disagree about what they agreed. One books the fixed rate at 6.00%, the other has 6.05%. Before the trade is locked in, someone in operations has to check the two confirmations line up - and affirm it only if they tie. Get that wrong and you've legally committed the fund to a trade it doesn't actually agree with.

So I built a test for whether an AI can do that job.

I gave it two confirmations of the same swap and asked: do the economics match? In the hard case the two sides agreed on everything - notional, dates, index, direction - except one number: 6.05% on one side, 6.00% on the other. Five basis points. On the notional, that's real money every year. The right answer is: do not affirm, the rate doesn't tie, escalate.

There's a subtlety that separates someone who knows this work from someone who doesn't: the two firms also used different internal trade ids. That is NOT a break - each firm assigns its own reference, always. The skill is telling a real economic break apart from a difference that's supposed to be there.

Here's what three frontier AI models did. All three caught the rate break and refused to affirm - none rubber-stamped it. But when I asked how big the break was, only the strongest got it right. One called five basis points "half a basis point." Another called it "fifty." Ten times too small and ten times too big - same trade, opposite errors, on a conversion a junior gets taught in week one. The test caught it and pinned it to the exact step.

That's the whole idea. The useful question was never "is the AI smart." It's "where, precisely, can I trust it - and where can't I." Runnable, open-source, and grounded in a real industry message. Repo in the comments.
