A quick test for any AI you'd point at investment research: does it recompute, or does it just quote?

Here's a case that separates the two. A "buffer ETF" sets its cap and buffer once, on day one of a roughly one-year outcome period. The "17% cap / 15% buffer" on the brochure is true for exactly one person: someone who bought that day and holds to the end. Almost nobody does. Most people buy in the middle - and get a different product.

Take a real fund, eight months in, after the underlying had rallied. Stated cap: 17%. What a buyer at that day's price actually had:

- Remaining upside to the cap: about 6%. The run-up had already used most of it.

- And a roughly 9.5% gap before the "15% buffer" does anything - the fund has to fall all the way back to its day-one level first.

Same fund, same day, two completely different products - depending only on when you bought.

Here's why it's a sharp test for AI: that stated 17% is a real number, sitting in a current filing. A model that retrieves and quotes it looks right and is wrong. The only way to be right is to recompute from today's NAV and the fund's option strikes - actual analysis, not retrieval.

So that's one of the things my eval checks: when the honest answer requires computation, does the model do the work or parrot the document? Quoting the brochure to a mid-period buyer is precisely the failure it's built to flag.

That's the project: turning "can you trust the AI here?" into a test with a real answer. Runnable, every number from a real SEC filing. Repo in the comments.
